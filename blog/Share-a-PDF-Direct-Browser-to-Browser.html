    The problem with "sharing" a PDF online

    Every time you share a PDF the normal way, your file takes a detour you didn't ask for.

    Email providers scan your attachments. Google Drive and Dropbox store your document on their infrastructure — sometimes forever. WeTransfer gives it a countdown timer. WhatsApp Web uploads it to a server before it ever reaches the other person. Even "secure" messaging apps handle your file bytes on their end before delivery.

    For casual memes and travel photos? Fine. But for contracts, medical records, tax returns, personal IDs, or any document meant for one pair of eyes — that's a problem. You're leaving a trail of copies on servers you don't control, governed by data retention policies you've probably never read.

    The tech behind it: WebRTC

    This isn't magic — it's WebRTC, a browser standard you've been benefiting from for years without knowing it. Every time you joined a Google Meet call without installing an app, WebRTC was involved.

    WebRTC establishes an encrypted data channel directly between two browsers using a process called ICE (Interactive Connectivity Establishment), which negotiates the fastest, most direct path — ideally a straight device-to-device connection, or a relay for cross-network transfers.

    →
    What the relay handles — and what it doesn't
    For connections across different networks, a TURN relay handles routing. But that relay only sees metadata — not your file content. Your file bytes travel through the encrypted direct channel between the two browsers. ihatepdf.cv's servers never see or store any part of the file.

    Frequently asked questions

    Q
    Does ihatepdf.cv see my file during the transfer?
    No. ihatepdf.cv's servers only handle the initial WebRTC signaling — the metadata needed to connect the two browsers. The file data itself travels through the encrypted peer-to-peer connection and is never accessible to any server.
    Q
    What happens to the link after the transfer?
    It dies. The link is tied to the sender's active browser session. Close the tab or finish the transfer, and the link is permanently invalid. There is no persistent file hosted anywhere.
    Q
    What if the recipient and sender are on different networks?
    WebRTC negotiates the best path automatically. A TURN relay handles routing for cross-network transfers — the transfer still works, though speed depends on both parties' upload/download bandwidth.
    Q
    Is there a file size limit?
    No server-imposed limit. The practical constraint is the available RAM on both devices and the speed of both connections for very large files.
    Q
    Does it work on mobile browsers?
    Yes. WebRTC is fully supported in Chrome and Safari on both iOS and Android. Both sender and receiver can be on mobile devices.
